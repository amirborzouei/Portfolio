Comparative LCA of a Paper Cup vs. a Reusable Ceramic Mug

This study compares the cradle-to-gate greenhouse-gas performance (Global Warming Potential, GWP100a) of two options for consuming one drink: (A) a single-use paper cup and (B) a reusable ceramic mug. The functional unit is one drink; results are reported per drink and scaled to 365 drinks (one year) for practical interpretation. The system boundary includes raw material production and manufacturing of the cup/mug, and the use phase (washing energy and water for the mug). End-of-life, transport, and other impact categories were excluded from the baseline for clarity; these can be added in sensitivity cases. Modeling was performed in openLCA using a simple CO‚ÇÇ emission factor approach and the IPCC GWP 100a metric to report results in kg CO‚ÇÇ-equivalents. Key modeling assumptions are documented in the parameters table (cup mass = 0.0095 kg; LDPE lining = 0.0005 kg; mug mass = 0.35 kg; mug lifetime = 500 uses; washing = 0.05 kWh and 0.7 L per wash; emission factors shown in the parameter list).

Interpretation
Per-drink baseline:
Under baseline assumptions the paper cup emits ~0.0105 kg CO‚ÇÇ per drink, while the ceramic mug emits ~0.0209 kg CO‚ÇÇ per drink (largely because washing electricity dominates the mug‚Äôs per-use impact).

Reducing washing energy or using a lower-carbon electricity mix dramatically improves the mug‚Äôs performance. For example, hand-washing at 0.01 kWh/use yields a mug break-even of ~56 uses; using a cleaner grid (0.1 kgCO‚ÇÇ/kWh) yields break-even ~66 uses.
