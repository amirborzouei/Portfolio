Study Project: Navigating EU Sustainability Reporting & Corporate Strategy

This study project, conducted at Brandenburg University of Technology Cottbus, Chair of Environmental Planning, as part of the Environmental and Resource Management (MSc.) program, provides a systematic review of the evolving landscape of sustainability reporting within the European Union. It specifically investigates how regulatory developments, particularly the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), influence the design and implementation of Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) systems and broader corporate sustainability strategies in EU-based corporations.

Core Objective & Focus
The central inquiry of this research was to understand the current status quo of EU sustainability reporting regulations and how these regulations influence corporate sustainable strategies. The project delves into the historical evolution of these regulations from 2014 to 2025, examines their current state, analyzes corporate adaptations, and discusses associated challenges and opportunities, while also looking at the future role of technology.

Methodology & Thorough Examination of Sources
This study employs a systematic literature review (SLR) methodology, adhering to the PRISMA 2020 criteria to ensure an open, repeatable, and objective review process.
• Data Sources:
The research drew from academic databases (Web of Science, Google Scholar) and official websites of institutions like the European Commission, Siemens, and TotalEnergies for policy frameworks and corporate reports.
• Inclusion Criteria:
Research focused specifically on the EU's regulatory environment for sustainability, ESG disclosure, or non-financial reporting, published from 2015 onwards, and included only peer-reviewed journal articles and credible official reports.
• Exclusion Criteria:
Non-English publications, studies published before 2015, materials outside the EU context, non-peer-reviewed materials (unless authoritative), and studies with no citations were excluded.
• Extensive Source Review:
The initial search yielded 428 records, which were rigorously screened. Ultimately, the study thoroughly examined a final review sample of 127 documents, comprising 94 articles from database searches and 33 additional sources from manual and grey literature searches.
• Data Analysis:
Qualitative content analysis was applied to recognize key themes, focusing on how EU sustainability directives (e.g., CSRD, NFRD, EU Taxonomy) guide corporate sustainability strategy, reporting procedures, and performance metrics, with particular attention to differences across sectors and over time.

Key Findings & Insights
The comprehensive analysis yielded several critical findings:
• Regulatory Evolution:
The EU's approach to sustainability reporting has systematically moved from voluntary and scattered practices to a unified and legally binding framework, with the CSRD significantly expanding obligations and introducing mandatory third-party assurance and digital formatting (XHTML). This replaced the Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD) due to its limitations in clarity, standardization, and enforcement.
• CSRD as a Catalyst:
The CSRD is identified not merely as a compliance framework but as a strategic driver, profoundly influencing corporate behavior, risk culture, and governance mechanisms by embedding sustainability considerations into formal risk management practices.
• Double Materiality Principle:
A foundational concept is double materiality, requiring companies to consider both the financial impact of sustainability risks and their broader social and environmental consequences. This principle is deeply embedded in both the NFRD and CSRD and is enforced through the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS).
• Transformation of ERM:
CSRD requirements contribute to a shift in risk taxonomies, strengthen internal control frameworks, and align ERM with stakeholder-oriented, long-term corporate sustainability strategies.
• Corporate Adaptations:
Companies are undertaking significant governance changes (embedding sustainability at the C-suite and board level), operational overhauls (aligning with ESRS requirements, decarbonization targets), and supply chain management strategies (rigorous due diligence for Scope 3 emissions and human rights). Innovation is also emerging as a strategic response to these regulations.
• Sectoral Impacts & Variability:
The study highlights varied responses across industries, noting that uniform regulatory pressure yields differentiated organizational responses depending on a sector's environmental footprint, operational complexity, and stakeholder expectations. Case examples from TotalEnergies, Siemens, and Volkswagen illustrate these adaptations and varying levels of maturity in integration.
• Challenges in Compliance:
Significant hurdles include data quality (especially for Scope 3 emissions and from SMEs), the complexity of new regulations and concepts like double materiality, and considerable cost implications. The risk of greenwashing is also a persistent concern.
• Strategic Opportunities:
Compliance presents opportunities to enhance stakeholder trust and reputation, improve access to sustainable finance (e.g., through EU Taxonomy alignment), strengthen risk management, and act as a powerful catalyst for innovation in sustainable technologies and practices.
• Role of Technology:
Artificial Intelligence (AI) and blockchain are identified as crucial technologies for enhancing the efficiency, accuracy, and transparency of sustainability reporting by automating data collection and analysis, improving data integrity, and streamlining supply chain transparency.
